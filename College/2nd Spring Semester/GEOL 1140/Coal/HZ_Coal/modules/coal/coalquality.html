Many factors play a role in determining the value of a coal. The two factors with the greatest influence on coal price are heating value and sulfur content.

Heating Value

Since most coals are burned to produce heat or make steam, the heating value of a coal has the most direct importance in determining its value. As a result, coal buyers are willing to pay higher prices for coals that have higher heating values.

The system of measurement used for the heating value of a coal is the British Thermal Unit, abbreviated as BTU. One BTU is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of one pound of water by one degree Fahrenheit at sea level. Coals range in heating value from about 5,000 BTUs per pound for lower-rank coals such as lignite, up to about 15,000 BTUs per pound for higher-rank coals such as anthracite.

Sulfur Content

Sulfur is an element that is contained within all coals. When the coal is burned, most of this sulfur is released into the atmosphere where it is an undesirable contaminant associated with acid rain. Government regulations place strict limits on the amount of sulfur that a power plant can release while burning coal. As a result, coal buyers are willing to pay more for coals that have a low sulfur content. This enables them to burn the coal and not have to spend large amounts of money cleaning their emissions. It also keeps them in compliance with the law.
