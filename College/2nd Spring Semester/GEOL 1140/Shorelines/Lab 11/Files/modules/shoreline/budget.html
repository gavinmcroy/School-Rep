Sediment Budget

The "sediment budget" of a shoreline is the balance between sediment added to the coastal system and sediment removed from the coastal system. When more material is added than is removed, a surplus of sediment exists and the shoreline builds seaward through deposition. On the other hand, when more material is removed than is added, a deficit exists and the shoreline retreats landward through erosion.

Sediment can be added to the budget through the erosion of beaches or cliffs, streams dumping their load at the shoreline and currents transporting sediment in from other areas. Sometimes communities modify the sediment budget of a shoreline, removing sediment from areas of natural surplus and returning it to the shoreline where erosion is a problem. The goal is to maintain a stable shoreline so that current land uses can be preserved.

A map summarizing shoreline stability for the United States was shown in the "Orientation." This map clearly conveyed that most US shorelines are unstable. People resist this instability with practices that sometimes successfully and sometimes unsuccessfully control the shoreline environment.

Case Study:
Beach Nourishment

In the last section we related the processes of erosion and deposition to the velocity of the water. This was an oversimplification because sediments added to or subtracted from the coastal system were not considered.

We can visualize the results of sediment addition by returning to our jar experiment. If the water in the jar is being stirred and carrying a full load of sediment then dumping more sediment into the water will result in deposition. This is because moving water can only carry so much sediment. Adding more sediment will choke the system and deposition will occur.

This gives us a third principle of shoreline erosion and deposition:

3) additions of sediment to the coastal system can cause deposition

Some coastal communities take advantage of this principle. If they have a shoreline erosion problem they simply dump sand into the coastal system. If the sand is added faster than the longshore current can carry it away then deposition will occur. Beaches down current from where the sand is added will grow seaward as an excess of sand is delivered and deposited. This is not a one-time effort because the growth, stability or erosion of the shoreline is now determined by the amount of sediment that people provide.

These situations tie the sediment budget of the beach to the financial budget of the shoreline community. Stability is not guaranteed!

Case Study: Seawalls and Revetments

The opposite of beach nourishment is a subtraction of sediment from the coastal system. People can subtract sediment by dredging or by making it inaccessible to the forces of erosion. Building a seawall or a revetment (a cover of erosion resistant material placed on a shoreline for protection against strong waves or currents) reduce the sediment supply of a coastal system.

In these situations, erosion is reduced where the structure is built, however, the longshore current still has the same sediment carrying capacity. If it can not erode an adequate amount of sediment in the protected area it will try to pick up sediment in adjacent areas. The result is a decrease in the rate of erosion in one area and an increase in the rate of erosion in an adjacent area.

This gives us a fourth principle of shoreline erosion and deposition;

4) subtractions of sediment from the coastal system can cause erosion in another area

These four principles can be used to better understand a variety of shoreline situations.
