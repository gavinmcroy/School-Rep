Streambank Erosion

In addition to erosion along the shorelines of oceans and lakes, erosion can also be a problem for streambank properties. The force of flowing water, especially during floods, can cause severe and rapid erosion. Streambank erosion can also be caused by wind-driven waves and boat wakes.

Efforts to protect the streambanks from erosion usually involve placing rip-rap on the streambanks. Rip-rap is a covering of rock placed on the streambank to protect it from erosion. To be effective, each piece of rock must be large enough that it will not be moved by the highest current velocity expected in that stream at that location.

Case Study: Stream Meanders

Bends in the river are very common locations for erosion caused by stream currents. As water moves around the bend its velocity decreases on the inside of the bend and increases on the outside of the bend.

The decrease in velocity on the inside of a bend causes the water to drop suspended sediments. This produces a sediment deposit known as a "point bar" on the inside of the river bend. The brown stippled areas on the map at right are point bar deposits.

On the outside of a bend the velocity of water increases. This increase in speed gives the water an increased ability to pick up and carry sediment. As a result, erosion occurs.

Over a long period of time, continued erosion on the outside of the bend and continued point bar building causes the stream channel to migrate across the landscape. This migration is known as "meandering" - a word selected for the seemingly slow and aimless movement of a stream back and forth across a flood plain.

Case Study: Island Migration

Islands have a special appeal to many people and an island residence or cottage is often viewed as a novelty. However, developing real estate on an island has some of the same concerns as developing any shoreline property. An additional concern is that many islands in rivers (and on shorelines) are slowly moving features. They are frequently being eroded on the side facing the current and growing through deposition on the downcurrent side.

An example of a slowly migrating island is Browns Island in the Ohio River between West Virginia and Ohio. Here the current of the Ohio River strikes the north end of the island and slowly erodes it. Deposition occurs on the south end of the island where the shoreline is protected from the current.

Browns Island can be seen on the map at right. If you click on the "enlargements" link below the map, you will be able to see the purple photorevision ink that has been used to represent a shoreline position that is different from when the map was first published.

Development on an island, especially on the upcurrent or downcurrent ends should be preceded by a study of historical maps or aerial photos. These can frequently reveal the rate and direction of migration that is occurring.
